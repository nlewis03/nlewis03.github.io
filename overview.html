Project Overview

Context, problem, goals, and target users.

Context

Why this problem is worth a quarter

Black women in the United States are more than three times as likely to die from a pregnancy-related cause than white women, 44.8 deaths per 100,000 live births compared to 14.2, according to 2024 CDC data. The disparity holds across every income bracket and education level, which points directly at systemic medical racism as the driver.

What these numbers mean, quietly, is that these women are dying because their pain gets dismissed, their symptoms go unrecorded, and their concerns are talked over.

What's already working

The Irth app pioneered crowdsourced reviews from Black and brown mothers. Zocdoc made provider search and insurance filtering more accessible. Postpartum Support International runs a helpline and provider directory. These are great pieces of infrastructure SpeakBirth can learn from and, in some cases, integrate with.

What's missing

No unified tool currently threads all three needs together: find a provider, fund the care, and evaluate the experience

Design goals

Three pillars

SpeakBirth is structured around three core design goals, each addressing a distinct user need that emerged from research.

Goal 01

Find care you can trust

Help Black mothers locate culturally competent OBs, midwives, and doulas — filtered not just by location and insurance, but by how providers have actually treated Black patients.

Goal 02

Know what you're owed

Help users understand and navigate their insurance benefits for doula care, postpartum mental health, and lactation support — without phone calls or jargon.

Goal 03

Rate your experience

Let users leave and read equity-focused reviews of hospitals and providers — built around what actually matters, like whether pain was acknowledged and concerns were heard.

Target users

Who SpeakBirth is for

The primary users of SpeakBirth are Black women in the United States who are currently pregnant, trying to conceive, or within the first year postpartum. This includes first-time mothers as well as those who have previously experienced dismissal or mistreatment during a prior pregnancy. Users may range roughly from ages 18 to 45 and may live in urban, suburban, or rural areas — though rural Black mothers face particularly acute access gaps.

Secondary stakeholders

Doulas, midwives, and community health workers who serve this population and may recommend the app to clients. Healthcare providers could also use aggregate review data to understand where they are failing their patients.

Key features

  • Provider search with equity-focused filters and ratings from Black patients
  • Insurance benefits navigator showing coverage for doula, mental health, and lactation care
  • Hospital and provider reviews using a structured equity rubric
  • Verified review system to protect against manipulation
  • Filtering by care type, time period, and rating
