Design Process

Research, personas, journey maps, storyboards, and iteration.

User research

What the sources actually showed

I conducted source-grounded research using NotebookLM with four sources, paired with hypothesis generation from Claude's Needfinding AI Agent. Comparing the two surfaced where the AI agent's guesses matched the evidence — and where they didn't.

Sources used

  • Source A: CDC and KFF maternal mortality data (2023–2024)
  • Source B: PMC qualitative study — interviews with 24 Black women on obstetric racism
  • Source C: STAT News coverage of Black maternal health tech, including Irth
  • Source D: Women's Health journal — mHealth perspectives from Black rural postpartum mothers

Six findings from the research

F1
Dismissal is the most consistent breakdown point
Sources B + D: Black women across income levels describe being dismissed and not believed.
F2
Nearly 1 in 3 Black women report mistreatment
Source A: CDC data shows 30% report being ignored or dismissed in maternity care.
F3
Peer networks are the trusted fallback
Source C: When providers fail, Black mothers turn to social media and peer reviews.
F4
Rural Black mothers face compounded barriers
Source D: Geographic isolation amplifies the effects of dismissal.
F5
Trust must be earned before functionality matters
Source C: Apps that center Black voices in design see higher adoption.
F6
Most maternal deaths happen postpartum
Source A: Majority of deaths occur between 1 week and 1 year after birth.

AI agent vs. NotebookLM comparison

The Claude Needfinding Agent hypothesized that users primarily need to find Black providers and that language barriers were a key constraint. NotebookLM, grounded in real sources, showed something more nuanced.

AI agent hypothesis
Users mainly need to find Black providers specifically. Language and literacy barriers are likely a key constraint.
NotebookLM evidence
Finding a Black provider matters, but provider behavior is the bigger issue. Language was not cited — trust was.
Comparison
Partially supported. The AI over-indexed on provider identity and under-indexed on how providers behave.
Design implication
Filters should emphasize "rated highly by Black patients" rather than just "Black-owned practice."
Personas

Who we're designing for

Three personas, synthesized from the research, capture the range of contexts in which SpeakBirth would be used.

TJ
Tanya Johnson
First-time, urban, Medicaid
Age: 27 · Chicago, IL
Context: 22 weeks pregnant. Felt dismissed at her first OB visit when her pain concerns were brushed off. Looking to switch providers but doesn't know how to find one she can trust.
Goal: Find a midwife who takes Medicaid and has good reviews from other Black mothers.
MR
Maya Reed
Second-time, suburban, private insurance
Age: 34 · Atlanta, GA
Context: Wants to hire a doula for her second birth after a difficult first delivery. Her insurance portal is impossible to navigate and her HR rep gave her conflicting answers.
Goal: Confirm her doula coverage and find a doula who takes her insurance.
JM
Jordan Miller
Postpartum, rural, recovering
Age: 30 · Macon, GA
Context: Six weeks postpartum after a near-miss when her blood pressure concerns were dismissed during labor. Wants to warn other mothers about the hospital and find a postpartum therapist.
Goal: Submit a verified review of her hospital and access covered postpartum mental health support.
User journey map

The current vs. ideal path

Tanya's journey today involves at least four separate tools, two phone calls, and several hours of work to switch providers. With SpeakBirth, the same journey could take under ten minutes.

Current journey (without SpeakBirth)

  1. Google "OB-GYN near me that takes Medicaid" — get a list of providers with no equity context
  2. Call insurance to verify coverage — wait on hold for 25 minutes
  3. Search Facebook groups for recommendations — get conflicting answers
  4. Look up each provider on Yelp — reviews are about parking and bedside manner, not equity
  5. Call each office to ask about availability — give up after the third busy signal

Ideal journey (with SpeakBirth)

  1. Open SpeakBirth, enter location and insurance type
  2. Apply equity filter — see providers rated highly by Black patients
  3. Read structured equity reviews on top providers
  4. Tap "Request appointment" with the highest-rated match

Tasks

Three core tasks

Building on the research, three tasks emerged as essential. Each maps to one of the three design goals.

Task 01

Provider search and filtering

The user enters their location and insurance type, then applies filters including equity rating, provider type, and distance. The app returns a sorted list of providers and the user can tap into any profile to see equity score breakdowns and individual reviews.

Task 02

Insurance benefits navigator

The user selects their insurance type and state. The app displays a clear summary of what they are covered for — doula reimbursement, postpartum mental health visits, lactation support — and links directly to a filtered provider list.

Task 03

Submitting and reading equity reviews

The user submits a review using a structured equity rubric (pain acknowledged, concerns heard, would recommend). Other users can filter reviews by date, care type, and rating.

Design iterations

What changed, and why

The early sketches and the final prototype look meaningfully different. A few key shifts:

From "Black-owned" to "rated by Black patients"

The first sketch of the provider filter included a toggle for "Black-owned practice." Research showed this was the wrong frame — what mothers actually need is to know whether providers treat Black patients well, regardless of the provider's own identity. The filter was reworked to surface equity rating instead.

From a long form to a two-question quiz

The first version of the benefits navigator asked users to enter their plan name, group number, and state. Sources showed that most users don't know this information off the top of their head. The flow was simplified to two questions: insurance type and state. Result: a one-minute task instead of a five-minute one.

From free-text reviews to a structured rubric

Originally, reviews were going to be a single text field. Research surfaced that the missing feature in existing tools is structured equity data — clear yes/no answers to whether pain was acknowledged, whether concerns were heard, whether the user would recommend the provider to other Black patients. The rubric was added so reviews are useful at a glance, not just in detail.
